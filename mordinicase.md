## 1. Executive Summary

Big Dawg (“Terry”) worked for Mordini Law, P.C. — a small litigation defense firm in Northfield, Illinois, whose sole attorney is Mark Mordini — performing core, ongoing, operationally critical work from no later than **November 1, 2024**, and continuing into 2026. Throughout that period, the firm and Mark personally failed to pay agreed wages on time, in full, or at all across a long series of pay cycles that are now documented by contemporaneous iMessage records.

The 65-screenshot text thread covering November 1, 2024 through March 17, 2025 establishes that (a) payroll failures were chronic, not occasional; (b) Mark himself repeatedly acknowledged in writing that Terry was owed money and that the firm’s payroll was “fucked”; (c) Terry complained about unpaid wages, misclassification, and financial harm in real time; (d) on March 13, 2025, Mark attempted to discharge Terry in direct temporal proximity to Terry’s wage complaints, then conditioned release of earned wages on Terry performing additional work; and (e) Terry explicitly invoked the prospect of reporting Mark to the IRS and the Illinois Attorney Registration and Disciplinary Commission and stated he had already consulted counsel — protected activity followed by adverse action in the span of minutes.

On this record, the strongest claims are: unpaid wages under the Illinois Wage Payment and Collection Act (IWPCA); violations of the Illinois Freelance Worker Protection Act (FWPA) and/or misclassification liability; retaliation (Illinois Whistleblower Act and FLSA anti-retaliation provisions); breach of oral or implied compensation agreement; and quantum meruit / unjust enrichment as a resilient fallback. Expense reimbursement under IWPCA Section 9.5 is a new standalone theory supported by Terry’s documented fronting of firm expenses.

The most time-sensitive issue is the **one-year IDOL administrative-complaint window under the IWPCA**. Because today is April 19, 2026, every missed-pay event dated before April 19, 2025 in the text thread is likely outside the IDOL administrative window *unless* tied to a “final compensation” trigger at separation. The court-based routes (5–10 years depending on theory) remain comfortably open, but any administrative filing should be done with the separation-date theory clearly framed and with the most recent pay cycles identified.

## 2. Reconstructed Employment Timeline

### 2.1 Phase 0 — Established Working Relationship (By November 1, 2024)

By November 1, 2024, Terry was already embedded in the firm’s day-to-day operations. On that date Mark asked Terry about the “CIC check” (First Chicago Insurance retainer/assignment payment), Terry was going in to “grab the rest of October’s billing,” and Terry was asking Mark whether that pay period would be paid by Zelle or check. This establishes three foundational facts:

1. The working relationship predates November 1, 2024 and includes at least the preceding October 2024 billing cycle.
1. Terry was handling billing for the firm, meaning Terry had direct visibility into the firm’s principal revenue stream (the CIC / First Chicago retainer client).
1. Pay was being delivered through mixed informal channels (Zelle and check), which is a pattern that persists throughout the entire thread and carries its own legal significance (see §6.6).

**Why this matters legally:** The employment relationship is established as of the earliest date in the record, not merely as of January 31, 2025 as the prior draft hedged. For statute-of-limitations and damages-accrual purposes, every documented pay period from October 2024 onward is a potential claim increment.

### 2.2 Phase 1 — Recurring Payroll Failures (November 2024 – February 2025)

The text thread documents a near-continuous pattern of late, partial, and missed paychecks throughout this window. The following is drawn directly from the record.

**November 1, 2024.** Mark confirms the CIC check has not arrived, saying it was “supposed to be here today.” Terry is pursuing late invoicing. This is the first dated payment irregularity in the record.

**November 27, 2024.** Terry asks Mark whether payday will be Monday — already anticipating uncertainty about when pay will arrive. Thanksgiving is the following day.

**December 3, 2024.** Mark writes: “Will have your money today about 1:00. Need you here and filing things today.” Later that afternoon, Mark escalates: “Are you working today or not? Hello. Did you quit? … Your money is in your office.” Terry responds: “No I didn’t quit. I’m trying to find a way to pay my rent so I have somewhere to live. I just got done filing Haymer… **This is the 5th time my check has gotten fucked.**”

**This single exchange is the most important evidentiary moment in Phase 1.** As of December 3, 2024, Terry contemporaneously asserted — and Mark did not deny — that there had already been at least **five prior instances** of paycheck failure. That establishes a pre-November pattern going back into the fall of 2024 (and possibly earlier), which expands the damages window considerably.

**January 15, 2025.** Mark: “Terry, this will not shock you, but no money today. I’m downtown and am waiting for word to go get check.” The phrase “this will not shock you” is itself an admission that nonpayment had become the expected outcome rather than the exception.

**January 24, 2025.** Terry flags three personal outlays for firm-related SharePoint charges — **$123.84 fronted from his own funds** for firm expenses. He puts them on a running reimbursement list. This is an independent expense-reimbursement claim (see §5.7).

**January 27, 2025.** Mark asks Terry to send personal identification information, including home address, phone, DOB (04/24/1989), and tax ID (36-4165056). The tax ID submitted is an EIN format, indicating that Mark was treating Terry as a 1099 independent contractor and preparing to send a W-9 to an opposing attorney. This is the key documentary anchor for the misclassification theory (see §5.2).

**January 31, 2025.** Mark: “Terry, of course payroll is fucked! There was $2500 in account 2 days ago. I checked this morning and now under $500… **Broken record.** Hope to pay you Monday.” The “broken record” self-description is essentially Mark admitting that the pattern of payroll failure is routine.

**February 1, 2025.** Mark manages only a partial payment: “Was able to get $500. In your drawer.” This partial-pay pattern undermines any defense that payments were simply delayed; partial payments show the firm was actively making choices about how much to release and when.

**February 4–5, 2025.** Mark: “Check situation sucks! Still trying to get answers. Will have to go there tomorrow on limited gas!! Need to deposit something tomorrow so I can pay you Thursday!!” The next day, Mark adds: “So are you on strike? Can’t get paid if don’t send discovery please.” Terry responds that he is being fined for late rent and — critically — **“about to be hit by the tax man for being misclassified as a contractor.”** Terry’s real-time assertion of misclassification is a significant fact for the FWPA / misclassification analysis.

**February 6, 2025.** Terry reports his own rent has bounced as a downstream consequence of delayed pay. Mark: “I’m freaking out. No discovery has been filed. I have 1900 for you. Be careful. **Our rent bounced.**” Both Terry’s and the firm’s rent bounce the same day, which cuts strongly against any later defense premised on the firm’s ostensible financial health but is also corroborative of the cash-flow chaos Mark created.

**February 14, 2025.** Another partial/irregular payment: “Money in your drawer. 💙”

### 2.3 Phase 2 — The March 13, 2025 Blowup (Threatened Discharge + Retaliation)

This is the single most important sequence in the entire record for purposes of the retaliation claim and, potentially, constructive or actual discharge.

**March 10, 2025.** Terry tells Mark he needs his pay today — “Literally cannot” go another day. **“Please stop fucking with me and ruining my life.”** This is a protected-activity wage complaint in plain English.

**March 11, 2025.** Terry continues: “Am I getting my money today? … **Mark you need to pay me.**” Later that day: “Like the schmuck I’m still doing your discovery and pleadings I have fyi.” Terry is performing protected activity (asserting unpaid wages) while continuing to perform firm work.

**March 13, 2025 (morning).** Mark detonates the relationship:

- Mark: **“What the fuck is wrong with you? Are you insane. Bank is not open. I’ll pay you up to today and you’re done. Get your money and get your shit out of my office today.”**
- Terry: “I’m on my way. Hope you thought this through.”
- Mark: “Is that a threat?”
- Terry: “No, you just literally never think past 5 minutes down the line.”
- Mark: **“Bring back all the documents you have please. No money for you today. Password needs to be fixed first.”**
- Terry: **“Ok if you’re threatening to further steal my wages, I’ll just go to IRS and ARDC today. I’ve already spoken to an attorney. I’ll switch it in front of you when you hand me my stolen wages.”**

**Within the same exchange, three discrete adverse actions occur in direct temporal proximity to Terry’s protected activity:**

1. Mark announces termination (“you’re done”).
1. Mark withholds earned wages (“no money for you today”).
1. Mark conditions payment of already-earned wages on Terry performing additional work (fixing the email password).

Terry’s response invokes at least four distinct protected channels: the IRS (tax/misclassification reporting), the ARDC (attorney discipline complaint), retained counsel, and an implicit wage-theft allegation. Under the Illinois Whistleblower Act (740 ILCS 174/), the IWPCA retaliation provisions (820 ILCS 115/14(c)), and the FLSA’s anti-retaliation provisions (29 U.S.C. § 215(a)(3)), this sequence is as close to a textbook retaliation fact pattern as one sees in practice.

**March 13, 2025 (later).** Mark reverses: “I have your money and plenty of work for you.” Terry returns. The termination is de facto rescinded. Terry subsequently resets Mark’s email password (a password Terry himself had set to “Payt3rryn0w” — i.e., “Pay Terry now” — in an earlier exchange, demonstrating that the wage issue had been actively communicated to Mark through the firm’s own systems).

**Why the rescission does not save Mark.** The adverse actions — threatened termination, withholding of earned wages, and conditioning pay on additional work — are actionable even if the termination was subsequently walked back. Illinois courts and the Department of Labor recognize that temporary, recanted, or partial adverse actions can still support retaliation claims where the employer’s intent to chill protected activity is evident.

### 2.4 Phase 3 — Role Expansion into Case Systems and Infrastructure (Feb–Jun 2025)

Beyond the text thread, the broader record reflects continued expansion of Terry’s operational role through mid-2025:

**February 15, 2025:** Terry was tracking case activity status — pleadings, discovery, and deadlines — and planning integration of Mark’s Outlook calendar with the JEFF list and notifications system.

**March 22, 2025:** Terry was configuring OAuth2 with Microsoft Graph API to access markmordini@mordinilaw.com. This confirms deep administrative access to and responsibility for the firm’s core email infrastructure — which the text thread corroborates, given the number of times Terry had to reset Mark’s password or walk him through Authenticator.

**June 28, 2025:** Terry was building a dashboard to display real-time case-related events from Mark’s Outlook calendar and match them to SharePoint cases. This confirms the relationship was still ongoing well into mid-2025 and that the firm was continuing to receive the direct benefit of Terry’s labor.

**Why this matters legally:** Every one of these facts reinforces employee classification and makes any contractor defense implausible. Terry was not performing a discrete project with a defined deliverable; he was building the firm’s operating backbone from the inside.

### 2.5 Phase 4 — Firm Obligations Still Active into 2026

**November 11, 2025:** The record reflects Terry stating that the firm works exclusively on the defense side — a routine operational fact, but one that confirms the relationship was still active 8+ months after the March 13 blowup.

**January 2, 2026:** Terry’s running master to-do list still included Mark-related items (Mark’s filing, Brito signature, other signature tasks). The firm relationship and its unresolved obligations were still active as of the start of 2026.

**Implication for damages:** Under the IWPCA, the 5% monthly penalty on unpaid wages accrues continuously while wages remain unpaid. If any portion of the wages documented in Phase 1 remained unpaid into 2026, the penalty on that shortfall has been accruing for 12–18+ months on top of the underlying balance. Similarly, a “final compensation” claim accrues at separation, and if Terry’s effective separation date is in 2026, the 1-year IDOL window may still be open for that final-comp trigger even if earlier individual pay periods are outside the administrative window.

## 5. Causes of Action — Ranked by Strength (Updated)

### 5.1 IWPCA — Illinois Wage Payment and Collection Act (Strongest)

The IWPCA (820 ILCS 115/) remains the anchor claim and is now substantially stronger than in the prior analysis because the text thread supplies the date-stamped missed-pay events that were previously missing.

**Core statutory framework:** Illinois employers must pay wages no later than 13 days after the end of the pay period for semi-monthly payrolls (shorter windows for other schedules). Final compensation must be paid in full by the next regularly scheduled payday after separation. A written contract is *not* required — an oral or implied agreement suffices.

**Why the case fits:** Every Phase 1 event in §2.2 is a candidate IWPCA violation. Mark’s “broken record” admission, the repeated partial payments, and the conditioning of pay on work performance (Feb 5 and Mar 13) are all independent IWPCA problems.

**Penalty provisions:** 5% of the underpayment per month while unpaid (820 ILCS 115/14(a)). On a hypothetical $15,000 unpaid balance owed for 15 months, that is $11,250 in statutory penalty alone, stacking on top of the underlying wage recovery. Illinois also authorizes personal liability for officers and agents who knowingly permit the violation — directly applicable to Mark (see §6.2). Attorney’s fees are recoverable by a prevailing employee.

**Most immediate timing pressure:** The IDOL administrative complaint window is 1 year from when wages were due (820 ILCS 115/11). As of April 19, 2026, every Phase 1 missed-pay event documented in the text thread is likely outside that window as a standalone pay-period claim. The path forward runs through the “final compensation” trigger and/or the court-based route (see §6.3).

### 5.2 Misclassification / Freelance Worker Protection Act (Now Central)

This theory has been upgraded from “conditional” to central because the text thread contains both Terry’s real-time misclassification assertion and Mark’s documentary treatment of Terry as a 1099 contractor.

**Documentary support:**

- January 27, 2025: Mark requests Terry’s name, address, DOB, and tax ID for a W-9 to attorney Keating. Terry submits tax ID 36-4165056 (EIN format).
- February 5, 2025: Terry tells Mark, in writing, that he is “about to be hit by the tax man for being misclassified as a contractor.”

**Classification analysis on the facts:** Under the IRS common-law test, the Illinois ABC test (as applicable), and the FLSA economic-reality test, the facts point squarely toward employee status:

- Terry was integrated into the firm’s core operations (drafting and filing discovery, managing the firm’s case docket).
- Terry used the firm’s systems (SharePoint, Outlook, Microsoft Graph API tied to Mark’s account, QuickBooks).
- The work was ongoing and open-ended, not project-scoped.
- The firm was functionally dependent on Terry’s continued presence (the Mar 13 blowup and subsequent rescission demonstrates this).
- Terry did not market services to multiple clients or operate an independent business.
- Mark directed the work at a granular level (“come in tomorrow morning,” “file rejection now please,” “get these discovery cases filed”).
- Pay was through irregular informal channels (cash in a drawer, Zelle, checks), not through arm’s-length invoicing between two businesses.

**Illinois Freelance Worker Protection Act (820 ILCS 185/), effective July 1, 2024:** Even if the arrangement is characterized as freelance, the FWPA requires a written contract for any freelance work valued at $500 or more (individually or in aggregate over 120 days), mandates timely payment, and prohibits retaliation. On this record there does not appear to be a compliant written contract. That alone is an FWPA violation. The FWPA provides for damages equal to the amount of unpaid compensation, double damages for late payment, reasonable attorney’s fees, and costs.

**Strategic consequence:** Mark is in a trap here. If he argues Terry was an employee, he owes back wages, overtime (if hours support it), the 5% monthly IWPCA penalty, and potentially misclassification penalties. If he argues Terry was a freelancer, he owes FWPA double damages plus attorney’s fees on top of the unpaid compensation. There is no classification exit that is clean for Mark.

### 5.3 Retaliation (Now Strong)

Upgraded from “conditional” to “strong” because the March 13, 2025 sequence (§2.3) supplies the complete protected-activity-plus-adverse-action structure that was previously missing from the record.

**Illinois Whistleblower Act (740 ILCS 174/):** Prohibits retaliation against employees who, in good faith, disclose or threaten to disclose activity they believe violates law, or who refuse to participate in unlawful conduct. Terry’s March 13 statement that he would go to the IRS (misclassification/tax reporting) and the ARDC (attorney misconduct reporting) is textbook protected activity under the threatened-disclosure prong. Civil remedies include reinstatement, back pay with interest, compensation for damages, attorney’s fees, and costs. Recent amendments have strengthened the statute’s remedial provisions.

**IWPCA § 14(c) — wage-complaint retaliation:** It is a separate violation to discharge or discriminate against an employee for complaining about unpaid wages or cooperating with an investigation. The March 13 “you’re done” statement, made seconds after a wage complaint, fits this provision directly.

**FLSA § 15(a)(3):** Federal anti-retaliation protection that attaches to both written and oral wage complaints. The FLSA provides for reinstatement, back pay, liquidated damages equal to the back pay, and attorney’s fees. Under Kasten v. Saint-Gobain Performance Plastics Corp., 563 U.S. 1 (2011), oral complaints about wages are protected.

**Adverse actions to plead:** (a) threatened termination; (b) withholding of earned wages; (c) conditioning pay on additional work performance; (d) demand for return of firm documents in a manner designed to prevent further wage-related protected activity.

**Even with the rescission on the afternoon of March 13, the retaliation claim survives.** The adverse actions occurred. The wage-withholding lasted hours and was lifted only after Terry returned to work. The chilling effect on Terry’s protected activity is a cognizable injury whether or not the termination was formally carried through.

### 5.4 Breach of Contract / Breach of Oral Agreement (Strong)

The text thread establishes the existence of an agreement through performance and admission. Mark repeatedly refers to Terry’s “money” owed, promises specific payment timing, and lists amounts ($500, $1,900). A factfinder has no serious basis to deny an agreement existed.

**Strongest framing:** “Mark and the firm agreed to pay Terry for ongoing law-firm work at a rate Terry was entitled to rely on. Terry performed. Mark and the firm did not pay as agreed, repeatedly and materially.”

**Illinois SOL:** 5 years for oral contracts (735 ILCS 5/13-205). Comfortably open.

### 5.5 Quantum Meruit / Unjust Enrichment (Strong Fallback)

Still the resilient fallback. If Mark later gets slippery about the terms — “we never agreed to a specific rate,” “it was informal,” “he was just helping out” — quantum meruit keeps the case alive. The measure of damages is the reasonable value of the services rendered, which in this case would be anchored to market rates for paralegal / legal operations / IT administration work in Cook County over a multi-year period. On the scope of Terry’s actual duties (drafting and filing discovery, running the firm’s case management infrastructure, email administration, billing), market-rate anchoring is generous.

**Illinois SOL:** 5 years. Open.

### 5.6 Illinois Minimum Wage / Overtime / FLSA (Still Conditional, But Closer)

Still dependent on hour counts Terry has not yet fully reconstructed. However, the text thread strongly suggests long, irregular, on-demand hours (evening texts, weekend work, holiday work, multiple same-day deliverables). If a reasonable reconstruction of weekly hours exceeds 40 for any significant number of weeks, the overtime theory activates and brings with it FLSA liquidated damages (effectively doubling the unpaid amount) plus attorney’s fees.

**SOL:** 2 years non-willful, 3 years willful under FLSA. The chronic pattern and Mark’s “broken record” admission would support a willful-violation argument.

### 5.7 Expense Reimbursement (New Standalone Theory)

The January 24, 2025 entry documents Terry fronting **$123.84 in SharePoint charges** for the firm out of his personal funds. There are likely other instances (the February 11, 2025 message references “a printout from Odyssey of all fees fronted” — i.e., filing fees Terry paid out of pocket — and “a list of reimbursement checks to determine what hasn’t been reimbursed”). This triggers:

**IWPCA § 9.5 (820 ILCS 115/9.5) — Expense Reimbursement:** Illinois employers must reimburse employees for necessary expenditures and losses incurred within the scope of employment that inure to the primary benefit of the employer. Filing fees, SharePoint subscription charges tied to firm cases, and similar out-of-pocket outlays for the firm’s benefit are reimbursable. The IWPCA penalty provisions and attorney’s fees apply.

This is a modest-dollar standalone theory but adds real weight when combined with the wage claim and when framing the overall pattern of financial shifting-of-burden onto Terry.

### 5.8 Personnel Record Access as an Evidence Tool

Unchanged from prior analysis. The Illinois Personnel Record Review Act (820 ILCS 40/) entitles Terry, on written request, to inspect and copy personnel records within 7 working days (plus a possible 7-day extension). Records to request: payroll records, pay stubs, timesheets, the personnel file, compensation agreements, disciplinary notations, 1099s and W-2s issued, and all correspondence regarding pay or employment terms. This is the cleanest path to the bank/Zelle/cash reconciliation data that will anchor the damages chart.

## 6. Expanded Legal Analysis and Strategic Commentary

### 6.1 The Employee vs. Contractor Question

The text thread is effectively dispositive of this issue against Mark. The facts supporting employee status are now overwhelming (see §5.2). The facts supporting contractor status are essentially limited to (a) Mark’s issuance of a W-9 / treatment of Terry as 1099, and (b) Terry’s submission of an EIN — both of which are formalistic labels that do not control the substantive classification analysis under any relevant test.

Illinois law and federal law are clear that labeling is not dispositive. The Illinois Department of Labor Handbook Interpretations, the Illinois Employee Classification Act (820 ILCS 185/ — in the construction context but instructive on Illinois’s general hostility to misclassification), and the federal economic-reality test all prioritize the substance of the relationship over the paperwork.

### 6.2 Personal Liability for Mark Mordini

**IWPCA § 13 (820 ILCS 115/13):** Officers, agents, and other persons who act directly or indirectly in the interest of an employer and who knowingly permit a wage violation are personally liable. Mark is the sole attorney and sole decision-maker at a two-person firm. He is the person who was physically placing cash in Terry’s drawer, personally texting about whether checks had cleared, and personally threatening termination over unpaid wages. “Knowingly permit” is not a close question.

**FLSA § 203(d):** The “employer” definition reaches individuals with operational control over significant aspects of the employment relationship. Mark’s role fits squarely.

**Practical consequence:** Terry is not limited to pursuing the PC entity, which Mark could dissolve, underfund, or wind down. A judgment against Mark personally reaches his own assets. Any settlement demand should name Mark individually as well as Mordini Law, P.C.

### 6.3 Statute of Limitations — Recalibrated

**IDOL administrative complaint (1 year from when wages due):** As of April 19, 2026, any individual pay period with a due date before April 19, 2025 is likely outside the administrative window when analyzed as a standalone pay-period claim. That encompasses every dated missed-pay event in §2.2.

**Three partial workarounds:**

1. **Final compensation as a unified claim.** If Terry’s effective separation from the firm is recent enough that it falls within 1 year of the IDOL filing date, a final-compensation claim under 820 ILCS 115/5 captures everything not paid by the next regularly scheduled payday after separation. On this theory, the “wages due” date is the post-separation payday, not the original missed-period payday. This is the most important framing choice because it potentially resurrects the administrative route for the entire historical balance.
1. **Continuing violation theory.** Courts have sometimes applied continuing-violation principles to serial wage violations, though the Illinois application is imperfect and case-specific. Worth raising, not worth relying on.
1. **Court-based route.** Illinois courts have treated IWPCA claims as subject to the 10-year written-contract SOL (735 ILCS 5/13-206) where an agreement exists, or the 5-year oral-contract SOL. The court-based route is comfortably open for every event in §2.2.

**Quantum meruit:** 5 years. Open.

**Freelance Worker Protection Act:** 5-year limitations period under the catchall (735 ILCS 5/13-205). Open.

### 6.4 Damages Multipliers and Penalty Provisions

**IWPCA 5% monthly penalty** on unpaid wages, continuous while unpaid. On a $15,000 balance unpaid for 15 months: $11,250 in penalty alone.

**FLSA liquidated damages** equal the unpaid overtime/minimum-wage amount when violations are willful. On a $5,000 overtime shortfall: another $5,000 in liquidated damages.
